# Mom Test Rules

Load in `customer-discovery` Step 4 / Step 5. Based on Rob Fitzpatrick's *The Mom Test* (2013) — the canonical rules for problem-discovery interviews that even your mum couldn't lie to you about.

## The 3 Core Rules

### Rule 1 — Talk about their life, not your idea
The interviewee must not know what you're building during the problem questions. The moment they know, every answer is shaped by polite agreement / disagreement.

Apply: lead with their day, their workflow, their pain. Save the solution reveal for after currency questions, if at all.

### Rule 2 — Ask about specifics in the past, not generics or opinions about the future
Past behaviour is data. Future-tense answers are noise. Forecasts of own behaviour are systematically over-optimistic.

Apply: never ask "would you", "could you", "do you think you would". Always ask "tell me about the last time", "walk me through", "what did you actually do".

### Rule 3 — Talk less and listen more
Founders dominate discovery interviews. Ratio target: founder talks 20%, interviewee 80%. Long pauses are gold — they trigger elaboration.

Apply: ask, then shut up. Repeat the last 3 words in upward intonation if they trail off.

## Bad questions → Good questions

| Bad | Good |
|---|---|
| Would you use a tool that automates X? | Tell me about the last time you did X. What did you actually do? |
| Do you think this is a good idea? | What's the worst part of doing X today? |
| Would you pay $30/mo for this? | Have you ever paid for something to solve this problem? What? |
| Is this a problem for you? | Walk me through the most recent time this came up. |
| How much would you pay? | What's it costing you today — in time, money, or sanity? |
| Would you recommend this to others? | Who else do you know who has this problem? |
| If we built X, would you switch? | What made you switch from <previous tool> to <current>? |

## The 5 Strong Signals (in order of weight)

1. **Currency** — they have spent money, time, or political capital on the problem. Highest weight.
2. **Workaround** — they have built / hired / cobbled-together a specific solution that's painful. High weight.
3. **Emotional intensity** — unprompted complaints, swearing, frustration. Medium-high weight.
4. **Past attempts** — they tried product X, Y, Z and abandoned them. Medium weight (tells you what NOT to be).
5. **Referrals** — they know 3+ other people with the same problem. Medium weight.

## The 3 Weak Signals (discard for verdict)

1. **Compliments** — "Cool idea", "I'd totally use that", "You should build this". Code as `COMPLIMENT`. Discard.
2. **Generic complaining** — "Yeah, X is annoying" with no specifics. Probe or discard.
3. **Hypothetical excitement** — enthusiasm AFTER the solution is revealed. Code as `SOLUTION-TINTED`. Discount heavily.

## Anti-Patterns During Interview

- **Pitching** — describing the solution before currency questions. Stop yourself.
- **Confirming hypothesis** — "So it sounds like X is your biggest issue?" → leading. Replace with "What's the biggest issue?"
- **Jumping to the next question** — silence is uncomfortable but productive. Wait 3 seconds before re-prompting.
- **Defending current product / direction** — discovery is not the moment to argue.
- **Taking compliments seriously** — it is the founder's job to discount them.

## When to Pitch

Optionally, in the wrap (last 3 minutes) AFTER currency questions, AFTER you've extracted the past-behaviour data. Mark anything said post-pitch as `SOLUTION-TINTED` in synthesis.

If the interviewee asks "what are you working on?" early — answer briefly and pivot back: "I'd love to tell you, but I want to learn from your experience first — it shapes what I build."
